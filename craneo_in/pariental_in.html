PARIETAL BONE

The parietal bones are paired bones of the skull that form its superior and lateral walls. They lie above the parietal lobes of the brain and are superficially covered by the galea aponeurotica. They are part of the neurocranium, along with the frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid, temporal, and occipital bones. The parietal bone is classified as a flat bone. The majority of each parietal bone forms the calvaria (cranial vault), while the remaining small portion is a component of the base of the skull. The function of the skull and thus of the parietal bones is to protect the underlying brain.

Where are they?

They are located above the parietal lobes of the brain. They are part of the neurocranium, along with the frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid, temporal, and occipital bones.

Functions

The parietal bones, as we mentioned before, represent the portion of the cranial vault which in turn is called (calota), therefore its action in the same way as all the bones of the vault brings the functionality of protecting the brain.

Providing a hard and very resistant coating that in the same way provides support for the meningeal membranes that are responsible for covering the central nervous system and also provides great protection against any trauma. It also offers support for the scalp.

Anatomy

Border: Sagittal (superior), squamosal (inferior), frontal (anterior), occipital (posterior).
Angles: Frontal (anterior superior), sphenoid (anterior inferior), occipital (posterior superior), mastoid (posterior inferior).
Cranial sutures: Sagittal, sphenoparietal, parietomastoid, coronal, lambdoid.
External face: Superior and inferior temporal lines, parietal tuberosity, temporal fossa, parietal foramen.
Inner face: Grooves of the middle meningeal artery, groove of the superior sagittal sinus and groove of the sigmoid sinus.
